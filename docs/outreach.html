Chapter 01 Energy Markets

Can AI Catch Cheaters
in Electricity Markets?

With Martin Huber & Jeremy Proz

A machine learning algorithm can detect secret price-fixing in energy markets faster and more reliably than traditional investigation, by spotting patterns that are invisible to the human eye.

The problem. In electricity markets, energy companies sometimes secretly coordinate their bids or deliberately hold back power they could provide to push prices up. These cartels cost consumers and businesses billions, but they’re notoriously hard to catch. Traditional investigations are slow, reactive, and expensive.

What we did. We applied machine learning the same technology that filters your spam and flags suspicious credit card transactions to bidding records from the Italian electricity market. The algorithm scans thousands of bids and automatically flags suspicious patterns that suggest collusion, far faster than any human analyst could.

The novelty. We also developed novel signals to detect capacity withholding: when producers deliberately offer less power than they actually have available, creating artificial scarcity to drive up prices. These signals come from the day-ahead market, where data is more transparent and timely.

The finding. The algorithm successfully identified known cartel behavior in both test cases. This is a proof of concept: automated, data-driven surveillance could fundamentally change how regulators monitor energy markets catching cheaters earlier and at far lower cost.

Chapter 02 Market Geography

The Hidden Map
of Competition

With Julie LeGallo & Jean Dubé

Firms don’t compete along official administrative borders. Real competition follows its own invisible geography and mapping it reveals 47 natural markets that look nothing like the official map.

The problem. When regulators decide whether a market is competitive, they need to draw boundaries around it. Typically they use official regions cities, counties, provinces. But do companies actually compete across those lines? Or do they have their own invisible territories shaped by distance, roads, and local knowledge?

What we did. Using eight years of bid data from Quebec’s municipal road paving contracts, I built a network showing which companies competed with each other based on where they actually chose to bid. I then applied tools borrowed from social network analysis to find natural clusters: groups of firms genuinely fighting over the same turf.

The finding. We identified 47 distinct competitive areas that don’t match Quebec’s administrative map at all. Distance matters enormously: double the distance between two firms and you roughly halve the chance they’ll bid on the same project. Competition is intensely local but locally it follows roads and economic geography, not political lines.

Why it matters. Regulators using official boundaries may be misjudging market concentration: missing local monopolies in some areas while overestimating competition in others. Better market maps lead to better antitrust policy.

Chapter 03 Antitrust Policy

Did Intervention Work
or do they Keep Business as Usual?

Job Market Paper

Antitrust intervention changed bidding behavior in Québec’s road construction sector but the effects were temporary, not permanent. Breaking a cartel matters, but may not be enough on its own.

The problem. For years, road construction companies in Québec secretly agreed on who would win which contract a classic cartel that cost taxpayers hundreds of millions of dollars. When authorities launched an antitrust inquiry and dismantled the cartel, a key question remained: did that actually make the market more competitive in the long run?

What I did. I tracked bidding behavior across hundreds of Québec municipalities from 2011 to 2022 before, during, and after the antitrust intervention. To isolate the true effect of the crackdown from everything else happening in the economy at the same time, I used a statistical method that makes treated and untreated municipalities more comparable before comparing them.

The finding. Yes, behavior changed after the intervention bids became more competitive initially. But those effects were temporary rather than lasting. Over time, the market settled back toward patterns that resembled the pre-intervention era. The cartel was broken, but the underlying structural features of the market its geographic fragmentation, high barriers to entry, and small number of local players remained.

The lesson. One-time enforcement actions, however powerful, may not produce lasting competitive change. Sustained oversight combined with structural reforms that lower barriers to entry may be needed to keep markets genuinely competitive after a cartel is dissolved.

Why This Research Matters

Markets Are Not Neutral

Competition in markets affects all of us through electricity bills, the quality of our roads, and how public money gets spent. When companies cheat, we all pay. My research shows that these problems can be detected with new tools, that enforcement can help, and that we still have much to learn about making markets work for everyone.
