Energy Markets

Can AI Catch Cheaters
in Electricity Markets?

With Martin Huber & Jeremy Proz  ·  Forthcoming

An AI trained to spot suspicious patterns caught electricity cartels that took investigators years to uncover — automatically, and far faster.

The problem. Energy companies sometimes secretly agree to push prices up — either by coordinating their bids or by deliberately holding back power they could provide, creating fake scarcity. These schemes cost consumers billions. They’re hard to detect, and traditional investigations are slow and expensive.

What we did. We applied machine learning — the same kind of technology that filters your spam or flags a suspicious credit card transaction — to bidding records from the Italian electricity market. The algorithm scans thousands of bids and flags suspicious patterns automatically. It successfully identified two known cartels. A proof of concept that automated surveillance could catch cheaters earlier, and at far lower cost.

Market Geography

Real Competition Doesn’t
Follow the Official Map

With Julie LeGallo & Jean Dubé  ·  Working Paper

Companies don’t compete along political borders. Mapping real competition revealed 47 natural markets that look nothing like the official ones — and some regions are far less competitive than regulators think.

The problem. When regulators decide whether a market is competitive, they typically draw boundaries using official regions — cities, provinces. But do companies actually compete across those lines? Or do they have invisible territories shaped by distance and roads?

What we found. Using eight years of bidding data from Québec’s road paving sector, we built a map of where real competition happens. The result: 47 natural market areas that don’t match the official map at all. Distance is everything — double the distance between two companies and you roughly halve the chance they’ll ever compete. Regulators drawing the wrong boundaries may be missing local monopolies hiding in plain sight.

Antitrust Policy

When Authorities Crack Down —
Does It Actually Last?

Job Market Paper

Cracking down on a cartel changed behaviour — but the effects were temporary, not permanent. Breaking a cartel matters, but it may not be enough on its own.

The problem. For years, road construction companies in Québec secretly divided up public contracts among themselves — a cartel that cost taxpayers hundreds of millions of dollars. When authorities launched an investigation and dismantled it, a key question remained: did the crackdown actually make the market more competitive in the long run?

What I found. I tracked bidding behaviour across Québec municipalities for 12 years — before, during, and after the intervention. The crackdown worked at first: bids became more competitive. But over time, the market drifted back toward old patterns. The cartel was broken, but the underlying conditions — geographic fragmentation, few local players, high barriers to entry — were still there. One-time enforcement may not be enough without structural reform.

// Why This Matters

Markets Are Not Neutral

Competition affects all of us — through electricity bills, road quality, and how public money gets spent. When companies cheat, we all pay. My research shows these problems can be detected with new tools, that enforcement can help, and that we still have much to learn about keeping markets genuinely open.
